Background

This tool is built by Andreas Rydström, a registered dietitian based in Uppsala, Sweden. It grew out of a recurring pattern seen in clinical practice: a patient reacts to a handful of seemingly unrelated foods, and the shared thread — a FODMAP subtype, a histamine load, a cross-reactive allergen — only becomes visible once someone lays every food's traits side by side.

Why this tool exists

Food intolerance work is full of diagnoses missed simply because nobody thought to check — alpha-gal syndrome, oral allergy syndrome, secondary lactose intolerance from undiagnosed coeliac disease. Spotting the shared thread by hand, meal by meal, is slow and easy to get wrong; this tool automates that comparison in seconds, surfacing overlooked possibilities without replacing a proper clinical workup.

How the analysis works

Every food in the tool is tagged with the traits it carries — FODMAP subtypes, histamine content, allergens, GI irritant mechanisms, and more. When you select a group of foods, the tool counts how many of them share each trait and ranks traits by how common they are across your selection.

Traits are tagged one of two ways. Some use a fixed threshold per 100g, with a minimum typical serving size so foods only eaten in tiny amounts don't get flagged:

  • Lactose: over 1g/100g
  • Alcohol: over 0.5% ABV
  • Fiber: over 6g/100g (skipped if a typical serving is under 10g)
  • Fat: over 10g/100g (skipped if a typical serving is under 10g)
  • Protein: over 20g/100g (skipped if a typical serving is under 10g)

Bile Stimulant uses either the fat or protein threshold above, but raised higher — over 17.5g fat or over 20g protein per 100g, matching EU food-labeling "high fat"/"high protein" categories. That's why foods like sour cream, ground meat, and Greek yogurt are split into separate low-fat, medium-fat, and full-fat entries: the fat content genuinely changes whether the trait applies. Turmeric is the one exception, tagged for its known effect on bile release despite not meeting either threshold.

Other traits have no numeric threshold — they're categorical, based on established classifications rather than a gram cutoff: histamine (SIGHI levels 1-3), allergens, oral allergy syndrome, GI irritants, and FODMAPs (Monash University classifications).

Refined Carbohydrates works the same way: it's assigned by food type and processing — white bread, sugar, and other ultra-processed or refined-grain foods — not by carb content. Whole grains, legumes, and vegetables are never tagged with it, no matter how carb-heavy they are.

Fat and protein content are also tracked passively in the background: since they're rarely the direct cause of GI symptoms on their own, instead of competing for a top spot in the trait ranking, they only appear as a side note when extremely common across your selection (over 90% of foods) — a signal they may be worsening symptoms driven by something else.

Data sources

Histamine content is cross-referenced against the SIGHI histamine food lists. FODMAP subtypes are cross-referenced against the Monash University FODMAP database. Threshold-based traits (lactose, alcohol, fiber, fat, protein, bile stimulant) use standard nutrition data per 100g. Refined Carbohydrates is tagged by food type and processing rather than a database, loosely following the same logic as the NOVA food-processing classification. Everything else — allergens, GI irritants, cross-reactivity, alpha-gal — draws on clinical literature and practice experience, and is reviewed for accuracy before being added.

The food list is still growing, with new additions prioritizing foods common in European and Scandinavian diets.

Limitations

This is a triage and pattern-recognition tool, not a diagnostic one. It can't account for an individual's dose tolerance, cooking methods, food combinations, or the many other variables that affect real-world reactions. Shared traits point toward hypotheses worth investigating with a qualified health care professional — they aren't a diagnosis on their own.
