Background

This tool is built by Andreas Rydström, a registered dietitian based in Uppsala, Sweden. It grew out of a recurring pattern seen in clinical practice: a patient reacts to a handful of seemingly unrelated foods, and the shared thread — a FODMAP subtype, a histamine load, a cross-reactive allergen — only becomes visible once someone lays every food's traits side by side.

Doing that by hand, meal by meal, is slow and easy to get wrong. This tool automates the side-by-side comparison so the pattern shows up in seconds instead of a week of food diaries.

Why this tool exists

Food intolerance and sensitivity work is full of diagnoses that get missed simply because nobody thought to check for them — alpha-gal syndrome, oral allergy syndrome, secondary lactose intolerance from undiagnosed coeliac disease. This tool is meant to surface exactly those overlooked possibilities, not replace a proper clinical workup.

How the analysis works

Every food in the tool is tagged with the traits it carries — FODMAP subtypes, histamine content, allergens, GI irritant mechanisms, and more. When you select a group of foods, the tool counts how many of them share each trait and ranks traits by how common they are across your selection.

Fat, protein, and carbohydrate content are tracked too, but handled differently: they're rarely the direct cause of GI symptoms on their own, so instead of competing for a top spot in the trait ranking, they only appear as a side note when they're extremely common across your selection (over 90% of foods) — a signal that they may be worsening symptoms driven by something else.

Some traits, like bile stimulant, are only assigned to foods that clearly cross a clinical threshold — currently over 17.5g fat or over 20g protein per 100g, based on EU food-labeling "high fat"/"high protein" categories. That's why you'll see foods like sour cream, ground meat, and Greek yogurt split into separate low-fat, medium-fat, and full-fat entries: the fat content genuinely changes whether the trait applies.

Data sources

Histamine content is cross-referenced against the SIGHI histamine food lists. FODMAP subtypes are cross-referenced against the Monash University FODMAP database. Everything else — allergens, GI irritants, bile stimulants, cross-reactivity, alpha-gal — draws on clinical literature and practice experience, and is reviewed for accuracy before being added.

The food list is still growing, with new additions prioritizing foods common in European and Scandinavian diets.

Limitations

This is a triage and pattern-recognition tool, not a diagnostic one. It can't account for an individual's dose tolerance, cooking methods, food combinations, or the many other variables that affect real-world reactions. Shared traits point toward hypotheses worth investigating with a qualified health care professional — they aren't a diagnosis on their own.
